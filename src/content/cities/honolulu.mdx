## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Honolulu

- **Verify the Part 107 certificate, not just the claim.** The FAA's DroneZone registry is public. Any pilot working commercially in Hawaii without a current Remote Pilot Certificate is operating illegally — and your insurance won't cover work they captured. Takes 30 seconds to check.
- **Ask specifically about Class B airspace authorization.** Honolulu is ringed by Daniel K. Inouye International Airport's Class B airspace, and large portions of Oahu require LAANC authorization before any flight. Operators who don't mention this unprompted either don't know or don't care — neither is good.
- **Match camera payload to the job.** RGB is fine for construction documentation and basic roof surveys. If you're assessing solar arrays at a resort or commercial property, you want a pilot who carries a calibrated radiometric thermal camera and holds an ITC Level I thermography cert at minimum. Ask to see a sample thermal report, not just photos.
- **Check their experience with Hawaii's terrain and climate.** Trade wind turbulence, salt-air corrosion concerns for equipment, and sudden squall lines are real operational factors here. A pilot who's flown exclusively on the mainland will have a learning curve — ask how many inspections they've completed in Hawaii specifically.
- **Get the deliverable spec in writing before you sign anything.** "Photos and a report" means nothing. You want geotagged image sets, thermal overlays if applicable, measurement data, and a clear delivery window. The standard is 24-48 hours.

> **Pro Tip:** For post-storm insurance documentation — especially after the kind of kona lows that push heavy rain and wind across Oahu — ask if the operator has FEMA IS-1505 UAS Disaster Operations training. It's not universal, but it signals they've thought through the specific protocols insurers require for loss documentation.

## What to Expect

Drone inspection services in Honolulu typically run $500–$3,500 per inspection depending on asset type, complexity, and deliverable scope — a simple residential roof survey sits at the low end, while a full utility tower inspection with thermal analysis and annotated reporting pushes toward the top. Most jobs are scheduled within a few days of booking, with final deliverables in your hands within 24-48 hours of the flight.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote almost always means one of three things: no thermal capability, no liability insurance, or deliverables that'll make your project engineer wince. Don't negotiate on price until you've confirmed the cert, the insurance, and the camera payload. Rescheduling because you need to hire a second operator isn't a savings — it's a delay with extra steps.

## Local Market Overview

Honolulu's construction and utilities sectors are unusually active for a city its size — ongoing infrastructure work across HART rail extensions, aging commercial rooftop inventory from the tourism build-out of the 1980s and 90s, and a dense cluster of solar installations driven by Hawaii's aggressive renewable energy mandates all create consistent demand for qualified operators. The state's strict environmental regulations around coastal and protected land also mean operators need to be familiar with FAA Part 107 waivers *and* DLNR permitting for flights near shorelines or state land — another reason to hire someone who actually knows the local regulatory landscape, not just the federal one.
