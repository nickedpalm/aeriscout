## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in San Antonio

- **Verify the Part 107 certificate, not just the claim.** Every operator will say they're FAA-certified. Ask for the certificate number and cross-reference it at the FAA DroneZone registry. San Antonio's proximity to Randolph AFB, Lackland AFB, and Kelly Field means large portions of the metro sit inside Class C and Class D airspace — only operators who actually know how to file LAANC authorizations will be able to fly those jobs legally.

- **Match the sensor to your job type.** RGB cameras handle roofing and construction documentation. Thermal (FLIR) cameras catch moisture intrusion and HVAC anomalies. Multispectral sensors are for solar array efficiency testing. An operator who shows up to your flat-roof moisture survey with only an RGB camera is the wrong operator — don't let them tell you otherwise after the fact.

- **Ask about deliverable format before you book.** "Drone inspection report" means wildly different things. Minimum viable: geotagged photo set with GPS coordinates embedded in EXIF data, annotated PDF report, and a thermal overlay if applicable. Some operators also provide orthomosaic maps or 3D point clouds for construction clients — useful if your project manager needs measurable data, not just photos.

- **Check insurance coverage, not just existence.** Texas doesn't mandate drone liability insurance, which means a lot of operators carry the bare minimum — or nothing — and won't tell you unless you ask. For any job involving commercial property, utilities infrastructure, or industrial facilities, you want a minimum of $1M in aviation liability coverage. Get the certificate of insurance before the flight, not after.

- **Get the turnaround time in writing.** Standard delivery in San Antonio's market runs 24-48 hours for a straightforward roof inspection. If an operator quotes you "7-10 business days" for a basic residential job, that's a workflow problem, not a complexity problem.

> **Pro Tip:** San Antonio sits at the edge of Central Texas's hail corridor — after a significant storm, Part 107 operators book out fast. If you're an insurer or adjuster working post-storm claims, build a vendor relationship before you need it. The operators worth hiring are not sitting idle waiting for your call in April.

## What to Expect

A standard drone inspection in San Antonio runs $500-1,500 for residential and light commercial roofs, $1,500-2,500 for cell towers, utility structures, or large commercial properties, and $2,500-3,500+ for industrial facilities, pipeline segments, or multi-site deployments with full thermal analysis. Most operators deliver within 24-48 hours for standard jobs; complex thermal reports with annotated findings take longer.

> **Reality Check:** The $299 "drone inspection special" you'll find on Thumbtack usually means a hobbyist drone, no Part 107 certificate, no liability insurance, and a folder of photos with no context or annotations. That deliverable is legally worthless for insurance claims and professionally useless for engineering assessments. Pay for the professional once or pay twice when the report gets rejected.
