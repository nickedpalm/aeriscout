## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Billings

- **Verify the FAA Part 107 certificate first, not last.** Any commercial drone pilot operating in Montana must hold an active Remote Pilot Certificate — not a hobbyist registration. Ask for the certificate number and cross-check it at the FAA's IACRA lookup. This takes 90 seconds and eliminates a significant chunk of the field.

- **Ask specifically about airspace authorizations.** Billings Logan International Airport sits right against the city core, meaning much of the urban area falls inside Class D airspace. A legitimate operator knows how to file a LAANC authorization before every flight in that zone. If they sound vague about this, walk away.

- **Match the camera payload to your job type.** RGB cameras document what you can see. Thermal cameras (ITC Level I or ASNT Level II certified operators) find what you can't — moisture intrusion, hot electrical connections, insulation failures. For roofing and solar array inspections, thermal is table stakes, not an upsell.

- **Check insurance minimums.** For anything beyond a simple residential roof, you want a minimum $1M general liability policy with a UAV endorsement. General contractors and utilities typically require this before a pilot sets foot on site. Request the certificate of insurance before signing anything.

- **Ask about deliverable format upfront.** A quality inspection produces geotagged photo sets, annotated thermal overlays, and measurement data in a format your team can actually use — not just a shared Google Drive folder of JPEGs. Reports should be delivered within 24-48 hours. If the timeline is vague, the process probably is too.

> **Pro Tip:** For infrastructure work in eastern Montana, ask whether the operator has experience with BVLOS (beyond visual line of sight) waivers or has worked with utility companies in the region. Operators who've navigated that paperwork have a fundamentally different level of seriousness than those who haven't.

## What to Expect

Drone inspection jobs in Billings typically run $500–$3,500 depending on asset complexity, flight time, and reporting requirements — a straightforward residential roof assessment lands near the low end, while a full transmission tower survey with thermal imaging and a formal deliverable package sits at the high end. Most operators charge by the half-day or project, not by the hour, so get a scope-based quote in writing.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake buyers make is comparing quotes without specifying deliverables. A $600 quote with raw footage and a $1,200 quote with a full annotated thermal report are not the same product. Nail down exactly what you're getting before you sign off on the number.
