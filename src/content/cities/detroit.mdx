## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Detroit

- **Verify the Part 107 certificate before anything else.** FAA Part 107 isn't optional for commercial UAV work — it's federal law. Ask for the pilot's certificate number and cross-check it at [faa.gov/licenses_certificates](https://faa.gov/licenses_certificates). Any operator who fumbles this question is already a red flag.
- **Match the camera payload to your job type.** RGB cameras work for construction progress documentation and general roof surveys. For industrial inspections of Detroit's legacy manufacturing facilities — corrosion mapping, heat loss on aging HVAC systems, solar array performance — you need thermal (FLIR) or multispectral capability. Confirm the sensor spec before signing anything.
- **Ask about airspace authorization.** Detroit sits under Class B airspace for Detroit Metropolitan Wayne County Airport and has overlapping restrictions from Coleman A. Young International Airport. Operators need LAANC authorization or a formal FAA waiver for many commercial jobs in the metro area. If they can't explain how they'll handle this for your specific address, they're guessing.
- **Check for thermography certification on thermal work.** Part 107 means they can fly legally. ITC Level I or ASNT Level II Infrared Thermography means they can actually interpret what the camera is seeing. These are different skills. For any job where thermal anomalies matter — utility surveys, building envelope analysis — the cert on the analyst matters as much as the cert on the pilot.
- **Get the deliverable spec in writing.** "We'll send you photos" is not a deliverable. A real operator will commit to geotagged image sets, a measurement report, thermal overlay files in a specified format, and a delivery window. Industry standard is 24–48 hours.

> **Pro Tip:** Michigan's industrial corridor along the Detroit River — refineries, automotive plants, port infrastructure — generates consistent demand for certified operators with OSHA 10-Hour and documented confined-space-adjacent protocols. If your project touches any legacy industrial site, ask specifically about their safety documentation history. The good operators have it ready.

## What to Expect

Commercial drone inspections in Detroit run $500–3,500 per engagement depending on asset type, flight time, camera payload, and report complexity — a simple residential roof assessment lands near the low end, while a full thermal survey of a cell tower or bridge structure with a written engineering-grade report sits at the top. Expect a site consultation or address review before scheduling, a 1–4 hour flight window on the day of inspection, and a 24–48 hour turnaround on the final deliverable package.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake buyers make is comparing quotes without comparing deliverables. A $600 quote that includes raw JPEGs and a $1,800 quote that includes geotagged imagery, a thermal anomaly report, and CAD-compatible measurement data are not the same service. Scope the output first, then compare price.

## Local Market Overview

Detroit's built environment creates unusual demand density for drone inspection services — a combination of aging industrial infrastructure along the river corridor, one of the highest concentrations of legacy flat-roof commercial buildings in the Midwest, and an active post-bankruptcy municipal renovation push that has general contractors working through deferred maintenance backlogs at scale. Michigan requires no state-level drone operator license beyond federal Part 107, but Wayne County project sites near transportation infrastructure will routinely trigger airspace coordination requirements that separate operators who've done this before from those who haven't.
