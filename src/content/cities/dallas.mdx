## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Dallas

- **Verify FAA Part 107 certification — and the actual certificate number.** Texas doesn't have a state-level drone licensing layer, so Part 107 is the floor. Ask for the certificate number and cross-check it on the FAA Airmen Inquiry database. You'd be surprised how many "certified" operators are flying on expired or recreational certificates.
- **Match the sensor payload to your actual problem.** A contractor needing construction progress documentation needs RGB and maybe LiDAR. An insurer dispatching after a hail event in Plano needs thermal overlay capability to find hidden moisture intrusion. A utility running a transmission corridor survey needs multispectral. Operators who show up with one GoPro-style camera and call it commercial inspection are not what you need.
- **Ask for a sample deliverable, not just sample footage.** Real commercial inspection work produces geotagged photo sets, annotated thermal overlays, and structured reports — not a highlight reel. If they can't show you a finished report from a comparable job, keep looking.
- **Check DFW airspace familiarity.** Dallas sits inside and adjacent to multiple Class B, C, and D airspace boundaries around DFW, Love Field, and Addison Airport. Experienced operators will have LAANC authorization workflows dialed in. Inexperienced ones will cancel your job the morning of because they "just found out" about airspace restrictions.
- **Get proof of commercial liability insurance.** Standard minimums for commercial UAV work run $1M–$2M per occurrence. Any operator working on construction sites, utility infrastructure, or insured properties without it is a liability you're inheriting.

> **Pro Tip:** For post-storm insurance deployments in Dallas — and there are a lot of them, given North Texas hail seasons — ask specifically whether the operator has FEMA IS-1505 UAS Disaster Operations training. It signals they've thought about safety and coordination protocols in chaotic post-event environments, not just clear-sky survey conditions.

## What to Expect

Commercial drone inspection in Dallas runs $500–$3,500 per inspection depending on asset type, flight area, sensor payload, and report complexity — a straightforward residential roof assessment sits at the low end, while a multi-tower utility corridor survey with thermal analysis and a structured deliverable package sits at the top. Most operators turn around basic geotagged photo reports within 24–48 hours; thermal analysis overlays and measurement datasets may take 3–5 days depending on the operator's post-processing workflow.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake clients make is comparing per-flight quotes without accounting for deliverables. A $400 flight that produces raw footage is not the same product as an $800 flight that produces an annotated, geotagged report formatted for your insurance claim or structural engineer. You're buying the report, not the flight time.
