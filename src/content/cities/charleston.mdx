## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Charleston

- **Verify FAA Part 107 certification before anything else.** This is the legal floor for commercial drone operations in South Carolina — not optional, not a technicality. Ask for the certificate number and cross-check it at faa.gov/uas/recreational_fliers/become_a_drone_pilot. Anyone who hedges on this question is telling you something.
- **Match the camera to the job.** A roof assessment after a hailstorm needs thermal imaging (ITC Level I or ASNT Level II certified pilots) to catch hidden moisture intrusion — RGB alone will miss it. Cell tower and bridge inspections need high-resolution zoom. Ask what sensors the operator actually owns, not what they can rent.
- **Check coastal airspace familiarity.** Charleston sits under Class C airspace from Charleston International Airport and has frequent temporary flight restrictions around the port, the naval weapons station, and during hurricane response operations. A pilot who's never navigated Charleston's LAANC authorization process is going to cost you time.
- **Ask for deliverable samples, not just a portfolio.** Geotagged photo sets, thermal overlays, and annotated reports are what you're actually buying. If a contractor can't show you a completed report from a similar job, you're their learning curve.
- **For insurance work, confirm FEMA IS-1505 training.** Post-storm loss documentation in South Carolina moves fast, and adjusters have specific documentation standards. Pilots with FEMA UAS disaster operations training know the protocol.

> **Pro Tip:** South Carolina doesn't require a state-level drone license on top of Part 107, but Charleston County has its own rules for operations near historic district structures. If your job is anywhere near the Peninsula — church steeples, rooftops in the French Quarter, anything within the Board of Architectural Review's purview — confirm your pilot has done this before.

## What to Expect

A standard commercial drone inspection in Charleston runs $500–3,500 depending on site complexity, sensor requirements, and deliverable depth — a straightforward residential roof assessment lands at the low end, while a full thermal survey of a solar array or transmission line corridor hits the top of that range. Most operators deliver geotagged photo sets and a PDF report within 24–48 hours, with raw footage available on request. Budget an extra half-day for sites requiring LAANC authorization or pre-flight coordination with air traffic control.

> **Reality Check:** The $199 "drone inspection" ads you'll see on local Facebook groups almost always mean a consumer drone, no thermal capability, and a Google Doc with some screenshots. The moment you need that report for an insurance claim or a structural engineer's review, it won't hold up. Pay for the certification; it's cheaper than a re-fly.
