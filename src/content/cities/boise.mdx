Finding a qualified drone inspection service in [Boise](/locations/idaho/boise/) shouldn't feel like a coin flip, but between the flood of post-pandemic Part 107 certificates and the handful of operators who actually know what they're doing with thermal imaging above a hail-damaged roof, it often does. ## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Boise

- **Verify the FAA Part 107 certificate, then go deeper.** Every commercial pilot in Idaho is legally required to hold a current Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate — that's the floor, not the ceiling. For thermal work on rooftops or solar arrays, look for ITC Level I Thermography Certification or ASNT Level II Infrared Thermography. A pilot without thermography credentials flying a FLIR-equipped drone is just taking expensive pictures.
- **Ask specifically about Boise airspace.** The Boise Airport (BOI) Class C airspace covers a significant radius, and portions of the Treasure Valley sit in controlled airspace requiring LAANC authorization. Operators who fumble on airspace questions — or worse, don't mention it — are a liability.
- **Check their deliverable format before you sign anything.** A geotagged photo set and a thermal overlay are standard. Measurement data, 3D point clouds, and orthomosaic maps are not — confirm what's included. For insurance loss documentation after hail events (common in the Treasure Valley's spring storm season), you want timestamped, GPS-tagged imagery that will hold up in an adjuster's review.
- **Match the operator to the asset class.** A pilot who specializes in construction progress documentation for residential builds is not automatically qualified to survey a transmission line corridor for Idaho Power. Ask for three references from the specific asset type you're inspecting.
- **Confirm insurance and waiver capability.** Commercial drone operations require at minimum $1M general liability. If you're working on a public infrastructure project in Ada County, some jobs require FAA waivers beyond standard Part 107 limits — ask if they've navigated that process before.

> **Pro Tip:** Boise's high desert elevation (~2,730 ft) affects battery performance and flight time more than pilots coming from sea-level markets expect. Ask operators what their adjusted flight endurance is at Treasure Valley altitudes — you don't want a rushed inspection because a battery ran short.

## What to Expect

Drone inspections in Boise typically run **$500–$1,500 for residential and light commercial** (roof assessments, small solar arrays, single-structure construction docs), with **$1,500–$3,500 for industrial, utility, and infrastructure work** requiring thermal imaging, multi-flight coverage, or specialized reporting. Most operators deliver geotagged image sets and a written report within **24–48 hours** of the flight.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote in the room is often the operator who will hand you a Dropbox link of unedited JPEGs and call it a report. Real deliverables — thermal anomaly callouts, annotated imagery, measurement data — take post-processing time. If someone is quoting you $200 for a commercial roof inspection, ask what the report actually includes. You'll find out fast.
