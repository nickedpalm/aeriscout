Finding a qualified drone inspection pilot in [Phoenix](/locations/arizona/phoenix/) isn't hard — it's finding one who shows up with the right equipment, the right certifications, and an actual understanding of what you need that separates a $500 invoice from a $3,500 nightmare. ## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Phoenix

- **Verify FAA Part 107 certification and check the LAANC airspace map first.** Phoenix sits under Class B airspace from Sky Harbor International, with Class D rings around Scottsdale, Deer Valley, and Chandler Municipal. Any operator who doesn't immediately mention airspace authorization when you describe your site is a red flag.
- **Match the sensor package to the job.** RGB cameras are fine for construction documentation. Thermal (radiometric FLIR) is non-negotiable for rooftop inspections, solar array surveys, and post-hail insurance documentation. Ask specifically: "Is your thermal camera radiometric?" Non-radiometric cameras can't export temperature data — they're effectively useless for anything beyond a pretty picture.
- **Ask for a sample deliverable, not just a portfolio.** Drone footage is easy to make look good. What you actually need to see is the inspection report: geotagged photo sets, thermal overlay methodology, and measurement data. If they can't show you a real report from a comparable job, keep looking.
- **Check for specialty certifications if your project is industrial.** For utility transmission lines or cell tower surveys, look for AUVSI CUSP or ASNT Level II Infrared Thermography. For post-storm insurance documentation, FEMA IS-1505 UAS Disaster Operations is worth asking about — it signals the pilot understands chain-of-custody documentation requirements.
- **Confirm your general liability certificate lists "drone operations" as a covered activity.** Standard GL policies often exclude unmanned aircraft. In Phoenix's commercial real estate and construction sectors, a certificate without explicit UAV coverage will get kicked back by your risk manager.

> **Pro Tip:** Arizona has no state-level drone registration requirement beyond federal FAA rules, but many Phoenix-area HOAs and commercial property managers have added UAV restrictions to their CC&Rs. Confirm site access rights before booking — a good operator will ask you about this upfront.

## What to Expect

A standard Phoenix inspection runs $500–$1,500 for residential rooftop or small commercial work; complex industrial surveys, utility corridor inspections, or multi-site insurance documentation typically land in the $1,500–$3,500 range. Most operators deliver geotagged photo sets and a written report within 24–48 hours, with thermal overlays sometimes adding a day. Flight time on-site is usually 1–3 hours, but budget for pre-flight planning, airspace authorization, and post-processing — the actual flying is the smallest part of what you're paying for.

> **Reality Check:** Operators quoting flat $199 "drone photos" are usually selling you footage, not an inspection. There's a meaningful difference between aerial photography and a documented inspection report with thermal data, measurements, and findings. If the quote doesn't mention a deliverable format, ask — or you'll end up with a Dropbox link full of JPEGs and no actionable data.
