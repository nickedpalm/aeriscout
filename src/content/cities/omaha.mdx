## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Omaha

- **Verify FAA Part 107 certification — then go further.** Part 107 is the floor, not the ceiling. For anything involving thermal imaging (rooftop moisture surveys, solar array hotspot detection), look for ITC Level I or ASNT Level II Infrared Thermography credentials. A pilot who only holds Part 107 and is quoting you on a thermal inspection is going to hand you a pretty picture, not a diagnostic report.
- **Check their airspace authorization history.** Omaha sits under the Eppley Airfield (OMA) Class C airspace, and significant portions of the metro require LAANC authorization or manual FAA waivers before any commercial flight. Ask directly: "Have you flown in Class C airspace around OMA before?" Hesitation is your answer.
- **Ask for a sample deliverable, not just a portfolio.** A polished video reel tells you nothing about report quality. Ask for a redacted inspection report — geotagged photo sets, thermal overlays with anomaly callouts, measurement data. If they can't produce one, they're not doing commercial-grade work.
- **Prioritize pilots with industry-specific experience for your use case.** A contractor inspecting bridge decks over the Missouri River needs different equipment and protocols than an insurer documenting hail damage in Papillion after a spring storm. AUVSI CUSP certification and OSHA 10-Hour Construction Safety indicate pilots who've crossed over from hobbyist into professional-grade operations.
- **Confirm insurance coverage.** Nebraska commercial operators should carry a minimum of $1M liability. If they balk at sharing their certificate of insurance, walk.

> **Pro Tip:** Nebraska has no state-level commercial drone regulations beyond FAA federal rules, which sounds like good news — but it means there's no additional filter weeding out underqualified operators. The burden of vetting falls entirely on you, which is exactly why credentials and sample deliverables matter more here than in states with stricter local oversight.

## What to Expect

Most commercial drone inspections in Omaha run $500–$1,500 for standard RGB documentation (rooftop assessments, construction progress, single-structure surveys), with thermal and multispectral work on larger assets — utility infrastructure, industrial facilities, solar arrays — pushing into the $2,000–$3,500 range depending on site complexity and report depth. Turnaround is typically 24–48 hours for standard deliverables; expedited reports after storm events can sometimes be negotiated same-day.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake buyers make is treating drone inspection like a commodity and defaulting to the lowest quote. A $400 roof inspection that produces 40 untagged JPEGs is not a roof inspection report — it's a drone flight. If your insurer, engineer, or project manager can't use the output to make a decision, you paid for nothing. Specify deliverables in writing before you sign anything.
