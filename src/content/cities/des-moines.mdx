## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Des Moines

- **Verify the FAA Part 107 certificate directly.** Anyone flying commercially needs it, full stop. You can confirm a pilot's certification number on the FAA DroniZone registry. Don't take a PDF copy as proof — take the certificate number and look it up yourself.
- **Ask specifically about Iowa weather ops.** Des Moines averages 34 days of thunderstorms annually and sees serious wind events off the open prairie. A pilot who's only ever flown in calm conditions isn't who you want on a cell tower survey when the forecast shows gusts at 18 mph. Ask how they handle weather holds and rescheduling.
- **Match the sensor package to your job type.** RGB cameras work fine for construction progress docs and roof condition reports. Thermal inspection — HVAC systems, solar arrays, electrical panels, post-hail moisture intrusion — requires ITC Level I or ASNT Level II thermography certification *plus* a calibrated thermal sensor. The camera and the human reading the data both have to be qualified.
- **Check their insurance, not just their cert.** Commercial UAV operators should carry $1M+ general liability with aviation endorsement. Iowa contractors and utilities have been burned by uninsured drone incidents — one rotor strike on a utility transformer isn't covered by a standard business liability policy.
- **Get the deliverable spec in writing upfront.** "You'll get photos" is not a spec. You want: geotagged image sets, resolution minimums, thermal overlay format (FLIR radiometric JPEG or separate data file), measurement data, and report delivery timeline. Reputable operators send a sample report before you hire them.

> **Pro Tip:** For post-storm insurance work in central Iowa, ask whether the operator has FEMA IS-1505 UAS Disaster Operations training. Insurers increasingly require documented protocols for post-event deployments, especially after the derecho seasons the region has seen in recent years.

## What to Expect

Most commercial drone inspections in Des Moines run $500–$3,500 depending on asset type, flight hours, sensor package, and reporting complexity — a straightforward residential roof assessment sits at the low end, while a multi-tower transmission line survey with thermal analysis and a full written report pushes toward the top. Turnaround is typically 24–48 hours for standard RGB deliverables, longer if the scope includes radiometric analysis or 3D modeling.

> **Reality Check:** The $99 "drone inspection" you see advertised on general marketplaces is usually a hobbyist with a consumer DJI Mavic and no commercial liability coverage. That price doesn't include actual analysis, a written report, or any accountability if the data turns out to be wrong. When the job involves a claim decision, a bid, or a regulatory requirement, the cheap option costs more in the end.
