## How to Choose a Drone Inspection Service in Jackson

- **Verify Part 107 certification before anything else.** The FAA certificate number is searchable at faa.gov/uas/recreational_fliers/knowledge_test_updates. Mississippi doesn't add state-level licensing on top of federal requirements, but that also means no state agency is policing who calls themselves a "commercial drone pilot." Do the lookup yourself.

- **Match the sensor package to your job type.** RGB cameras are fine for construction progress documentation and basic roof assessments. If you're doing HVAC or solar array inspections — and Jackson has more commercial flat-roof industrial facilities per capita than you'd expect — you need a pilot with a calibrated thermal camera and an ITC Level I or ASNT Level II thermography cert. A FLIR Zenmuse XT2 in the hands of someone who can't read a thermal overlay is just expensive guesswork.

- **Ask for a sample deliverable from a comparable job.** Not a portfolio highlight reel — a real report from a similar asset type. A good drone inspection report for a Mississippi transmission line survey looks nothing like a roof-damage report for a hail claim. If they can't show you what the output looks like before you hire them, that's your answer.

- **Check their airspace authorization experience.** Jackson's commercial and industrial corridors sit under or near controlled airspace from Jackson-Medgar Wiley Evers International Airport. Pilots operating legally in that footprint need LAANC authorization or waivers. Ask them to walk you through their authorization process — a pilot who's done it before can explain it in 30 seconds.

- **Confirm insurance coverage.** At minimum, $1M general liability with an aviation endorsement is the industry floor for commercial work. Some utilities and general contractors in Mississippi require $2M. Get the certificate of insurance before the site visit, not after.

> **Pro Tip:** Jackson-area adjusters dealing with post-hail and wind claims should ask pilots if they carry FEMA IS-1505 (UAS Disaster Operations) certification — it's a signal the pilot has thought seriously about documentation protocols under pressure, which matters when you're processing 40 claims in the same zip code after a severe weather event.

## What to Expect

Most commercial drone inspections in Jackson run $500 to $3,500 depending on asset complexity, sensor package, and deliverable format — a straightforward residential roof assessment sits at the low end; a multi-span bridge survey with thermal overlay and measurement data hits the top. Standard turnaround is 24-48 hours for the full report, though pilots working post-storm surge events in Mississippi have been known to deliver same-day preliminary photo sets for urgent loss documentation. The deliverable you're paying for is geotagged imagery, annotated findings, and — for thermal jobs — a calibrated overlay that's actually interpretable, not just a colorful JPEG.

> **Reality Check:** Cheap quotes often mean the pilot is skipping the post-processing step — raw footage handed over as a Google Drive link is not an inspection report. If someone quotes you $200 for a commercial roof inspection, ask what the deliverable includes. Nine times out of ten, the answer reveals they've never done one professionally.
